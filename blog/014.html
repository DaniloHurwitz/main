How to Write a Proposal That Gets Read (The Sniper Method)

You’ve seen the numbers: 50 proposals for a single job. Clients are overwhelmed by noise. If you want to land high-value projects at your expert rate, you have to stop sending "shotgun" proposals and adopt the Sniper Method. This means targeting fewer jobs but ensuring your proposal is impossible to ignore.

1. The 3-Second Hook is Everything

Clients see only the first one or two lines of your proposal. You must grab their attention immediately by proving you read the job post. Do not start with "Hello, my name is Danilo." Instead, jump straight to their pain point:

  • Generic: *I am an experienced medical translator...*
  • Sniper: *For your complex clinical trial reports, you need an MD student with active clinical knowledge, not just a linguist.*
  • Rule: Mention their specific project or a detail they included (e.g., "The specific challenge of Vancouver referencing...") within the first 15 words.

2. The $45/hr Justification (Risk Reduction)

If you're bidding a premium rate, you must explicitly justify the value. You are not selling time; you are selling certainty and risk elimination. Focus on the cost of error:

  • Pitch: "My rate reflects zero risk. A small mistranslation in patient documentation can cost thousands in delays. My medical expertise guarantees accuracy."
  • Proof: Briefly mention your 100% Job Success Score and Upwork Top Rated status.

3. The Structure: Be Concise, Not Generic

Keep your proposal brief (under 5 paragraphs is ideal):

  • Paragraph 1: The Hook. (See above).
  • Paragraph 2: The Proof. Briefly state why your unique background (MD student, Trilingual) solves their specific problem better than the other 49 proposals.
  • Paragraph 3: The Plan. Outline the next steps (e.g., "I will provide a custom glossary for consistency," or "I'm ready for a quick 5-minute call to confirm scope").
  • Closing: CTA. Ask a question about the project (e.g., "Could you confirm the total word count for the first manuscript?") to encourage a reply.

Final Thoughts

The Sniper Method is about high investment, low volume. It requires patience because you'll apply to fewer jobs, but when you do, your proposal should be so hyper-targeted that the client believes you're the *only* person who can do the work with zero fuss. This is how you win premium projects.
